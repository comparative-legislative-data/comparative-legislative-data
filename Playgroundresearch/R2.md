Good question to bring to an outside reader before you sink more months into it. Here's my honest take, organized around your four questions.

**Is it innovative, foolhardy, or reinventing the wheel? A bit of all three, honestly.**

The *data model* is not new — parliamentary bill-tracking with stage dates, roll calls, committee rosters, and point-in-time party/coalition coding is exactly what ParlGov, the Comparative Agendas Project, and newer efforts like ParlaCAP already do at scale. ParlGov is a well-established data infrastructure covering elections, parties, and cabinets across EU and OECD democracies going back to 1900, and a 2026 project called ParlaCAP has just built an LLM-based pipeline applying the Comparative Agendas Project coding scheme across 28 European parliaments and millions of speeches. So the "give researchers clean, harmonized, point-in-time legislative variables" part of your pitch is a crowded space, not a gap. Your Pass 1/Pass 2 empirical-baseline-vs-candidate distinction is a genuinely good discipline, but it's a research-methods virtue, not a product differentiator — reviewers will ask "why should I trust your joins more than ParlGov's or CAP's," and the honest answer right now is "no reason yet, it's a single-country pilot."

Where it *is* potentially novel is the delivery mechanism: a fully in-browser, zero-install, four-studio analytical environment (visual builder, spreadsheet/pivot, econometrics with LaTeX export, and actual R via WebR) running against WASM engines, with a crowdsourced "propose as preset" gallery. I'm not aware of an existing comparative-politics platform that combines DuckDB-Wasm + WebR + Pyodide into one browser-native lab with replication-code generation built in. That's the part worth prototyping first, decoupled from the question of whether your underlying dataset is differentiated.

**Pros and cons**

Genuine advantages: zero setup is a real barrier removed for the many political scientists who are not confident with the command line; instant reproducibility (code auto-generated alongside the chart) is a nice nudge toward open-science norms; no server hosting cost for compute is attractive for a small team, since the browser does the work.

Real risks: browser memory ceilings will bite as soon as someone tries anything beyond your Holyrood pilot (WebR and Pyodide are not lightweight, and running both plus DuckDB-Wasm in one tab on a laptop is a stress test); WASM R/Python are usually missing packages or running old versions, which will frustrate exactly the power users you're trying to attract; econometrics-by-drag-and-drop risks encouraging model misspecification by non-specialists who don't understand what OLS assumptions they're violating; and the "propose as preset" moderation queue is a real ongoing labor cost that a small pilot team may underestimate — crowdsourced galleries die quietly when nobody curates them.

**Landscape**

Beyond ParlGov and CAP, look closely at ParlaCAP/ParlaMint (NLP-based agenda and sentiment analysis across 28 European parliaments' full-text speeches) — it's the closest thing to a "modern, actively funded, computational" competitor in this exact space, and it's from 2026, so it's very fresh. None of these, as far as I can tell, offer an in-browser multi-studio lab — they distribute datasets and expect you to bring your own Stata/R/Python. That supports treating the interactive playground, not the dataset, as your unique selling point.

**Sequencing advice**

Your instinct (Visual Builder + Grid Studio → Econometrics/LaTeX → full WebR) is right, and I'd go further: don't build all four studios before validating that one. Ship Visual Builder + Grid Studio against the Holyrood pilot only, get five or six actual comparative-politics researchers to break it, and use that to decide whether WebR is worth its weight — it's the most technically expensive and fragile piece, and if your users are happy generating replication code without running it live in-browser, you may not need it at all. I'd also flag one landscape reality check before you go further: the Scottish Parliament's open API does expose bill data, so your Pass 1 sourcing claim checks out — but confirm now, at pilot stage, that the API's coverage and stability will hold up before you build four studios on top of it.
